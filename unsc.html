Committee Topic

Topic A: Checking the Expansionist and Totalitarian Policy of China

This topic shall encompass the crises in the regions of Tibet, the South China Sea, Taiwan and the Sino-Indian Conflict, along with the rising Totalitarianism breeding from strict censorship laws and the Dictator-like leading figures, an example of which is still in power, the Premier, Xi Jinping. These, along with China's strategic alliances with states following non-cooperation policies such as Russia and North Korea, pose a problem. Due to China's economic and political monopoly in Asia and its status as an economic superpower, the threat that it could evolve into a rogue state that heeds not the warning of any state, not even the United Nations, remains ever present. As we know from past precedents such as the Chinese occupation of the state of Tibet which has led to the death of over 1.2 million Tibetans, and human rights conditions remain dismal, China is a powerful state that cannot be allowed to run rampant. In Tibet today, there is no freedom of speech, no freedom of assembly, no freedom of press and no freedom of religion. Tibetan culture, language, identity, religion, economy and environment are all modified and tweaked in such a way to benefit China exclusively, much less Tibet as a whole, and the job condition has deteriorated, with Chinese occupants holding all the higher posts in government and there being a huge imbalance in the resources available. China is thus a state that must be checked and held accountable for all the various territorialistic aggression it embodies within its foreign policy, as well as its mass propaganda and censorship within its borders.

Topic B: Crisis in Somalia

Somalia is a nation situated in the Horn of Africa. It is flanked by Ethiopia toward the west, Djibouti toward the northwest, the Gulf of Aden toward the north, the Indian Sea toward the east, and Kenya toward the southwest. Somalia has a populace of around 10.8 million. Around 85% of its inhabitants are ethnic Somalis. Ethnic minorities are to a great extent moved in the southern areas. A great many people in the nation are Muslim, with the larger part being Sunni. Somalia was given independence by the British and Italy in 1960, one of the last nations to be in the United Nations Trusteeship Council. Civil unrest broke out in 1991, and this led Somalia to be declared a failed state for about a decade. Even after the establishment of interim governments in the years 2000 and 2004, Somalia still topped the world's charts in the Failed States Index (FSI) from 2008-2013. In the midst of this unrest, the Islamic Courts Union was founded, and after a crushing defeat, gave way to its offshoot, Al-Shabaab. This terrorist faction has since pledged allegiance to Al Qaeda and Boko Haram, the latter of which is a known ally of the Islamic State. The organization portrays itself as pursuing jihad against "adversaries of Islam", and is occupied with battle against the Government of Somalia and the African Union Mission to Somalia (AMISOM). Al-Shabaab has been labelled a terrorist association by Australia, Canada, the United Arab Emirates, the United Kingdom and the United States. As of June 2012, the US State Division has declared open bounties on a few of the faction's senior authorities. The death toll in Somalia due to both the civil war and the activities of Al-Shabaab is, since 1991, 500,000, averaging at 3000-4000 civilian casualties per annum, and a total of 1.1 million made either internally displaced or refugees.
